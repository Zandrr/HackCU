FAQ

  • What is HackCU?

    HackCU is the University of Colorado's first ever national hackathon. It is a beginner friendly place to learn and network with other hackers, makers, and builders. This is going to be the largest collegiate hackathon within 800 miles! Grab up to 3 teammates and let's see what you got.

  • When is HackCU?

    HackCU kicks off the evening of Friday, April 10th and ends on Sunday, April 12th. We will open registration at 4pm on Friday, with the opening ceremony beginning at 5pm, and the event will conclude at 3pm on Sunday. A full schedule will be published at a later date.

  • What Should I Bring?

    Everything you need for 36 hours of hygenic hacking. This may include a toothbrush, deodorant, a change of clothes. In addition, bring all your electronics: Chargers, laptop, headphones, powerstrip, whatever you need. Food and drink will be provided.

  • Am I Allowed to Use Past Projects?

    No. This is a competition to see what you can make in 36 hours from scratch! Although libraries such as bootstrap and API's are fair game.

  • What is the Cost of Attending?

    It's completely free. We'll provide food and all other associated costs for the entire weekend.

Judges

Gabe Johnson

Gabe received a PhD in Computation Design from Carnegie Mellon University and has since become a thriving member of the startup community in Boulder. With his passion for computational design, he is currently following his dream of forming a startup with a beginner-friendly CAD tool called Zotebook .

Fletcher Richman

Fletcher received his Electrical & Computer Engineering degree from CU Boulder the same year he co-founded Spark Boulder, Colorado's first student coworking space. He's now the managing director of Spark, and a Growth Engineer at PivotDesk, a TechStars company. He also helps build the Colorado startup ecosystem by putting on Boulder Open Angel Forum and BoulderBeta.

Alicia Gibb

Alicia Gibb is an advocate for open hardware, a researcher, and a hardware hacker. She is the Executive Director of the Open Source Hardware Association (OSHWA), CEO of Lunchbox Electronics, and director of the BTU Lab at CU Boulder. She is a member of NYCResistor, a hackerspace in Brooklyn, and has judged hackathons in New York both inside and outside of the university setting. When Alicia is not busy researching at the crossroads of open technology and innovation she is prototyping work that twitches, blinks, and might even be tasty to eat.
